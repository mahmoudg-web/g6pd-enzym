01   Definition & Overview

What is G6PD?

Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD) is a cytoplasmic enzyme classified as an oxidoreductase (EC 1.1.1.49). It catalyzes the first and rate-limiting step of the Pentose Phosphate Pathway (PPP), also called the hexose monophosphate shunt (HMP shunt). It is found in virtually all cells but is of critical importance in erythrocytes, where it is the sole enzymatic source of NADPH.

G6PD uses NADP⁺ as its cofactor, reducing it to NADPH. This NADPH is essential for regenerating glutathione (GSH), the primary antioxidant molecule that protects red blood cells from oxidative stress and hemolysis.

Why Erythrocytes Are Uniquely Vulnerable

Red blood cells lack mitochondria and nuclei. They cannot synthesize new proteins or use the TCA cycle. G6PD-driven NADPH production is their only mechanism to counter oxidative damage. Without functional G6PD, even mild oxidative stress causes irreversible hemoglobin oxidation and hemolysis.

03   Reaction Mechanism

Catalytic Reaction

G6PD catalyzes the oxidative decarboxylation of glucose-6-phosphate. The reaction proceeds in two stages and is irreversible under physiological conditions, committing glucose-6-phosphate to the Pentose Phosphate Pathway.

Step 1 — G6PD Reaction (EC 1.1.1.49)
Glucose-6-Phosphate + NADP⁺ →[G6PD] 6-Phosphogluconolactone + NADPH + H⁺
Step 2 — 6-Phosphogluconolactonase (spontaneous / enzymatic)
6-Phosphogluconolactone + H₂O → 6-Phosphogluconate
(hydrolyzes spontaneously or via lactonase; feeds into next PPP step)

Step-by-Step Mechanism

Substrate Binding

Glucose-6-phosphate binds to the active site. NADP⁺ simultaneously occupies the catalytic cofactor site via its nicotinamide ring positioned for hydride transfer.

Hydride Transfer (Oxidation Step)

A hydride ion (H⁻) is transferred from C-1 of glucose-6-phosphate to the nicotinamide ring of NADP⁺. This oxidizes G6P to the lactone form and reduces NADP⁺ → NADPH.

6-Phosphogluconolactone Release

6-Phosphogluconolactone is released. NADPH dissociates and is replaced by a fresh NADP⁺ molecule for the next catalytic cycle.

Lactonase Hydrolysis

6-Phosphogluconolactone is hydrolyzed to 6-Phosphogluconate — either spontaneously or by 6-phosphogluconolactonase — entering the next enzymatic step of the PPP.

04   Pentose Phosphate Pathway

Role in the PPP & Antioxidant Defense

The Pentose Phosphate Pathway has two phases: the oxidative phase (where G6PD acts) and the non-oxidative phase. G6PD is the gateway enzyme and produces all NADPH generated by this pathway.

The NADPH → GSH Axis

NADPH (from G6PD)  →  Glutathione Reductase uses NADPH to regenerate GSH from GSSG  →  Glutathione Peroxidase uses GSH to neutralize H₂O₂ and lipid peroxides  →  Erythrocyte membrane and hemoglobin are protected. Without G6PD, this entire chain collapses.

📌

Other PPP Products — Ribose-5-Phosphate

The non-oxidative phase produces ribose-5-phosphate, essential for nucleotide and nucleic acid synthesis. The PPP is therefore critical for both antioxidant defense (via NADPH) and cellular proliferation (via ribose-5-phosphate). This explains why G6PD is highly expressed in rapidly dividing cells, including cancer cells.

Pathophysiology of Hemolysis in G6PD Deficiency

Oxidative Trigger

Exposure to an oxidative trigger (drug, infection, fava beans, metabolic stress) generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) and H₂O₂ within the erythrocyte.

NADPH Depletion

Deficient G6PD cannot regenerate sufficient NADPH. The glutathione antioxidant system fails as GSH cannot be replenished from GSSG.

Hemoglobin Oxidation → Heinz Bodies

Oxidized hemoglobin (methemoglobin) denatures and precipitates as Heinz bodies — intracellular inclusions visible on supravital stain. These attach to the inner cell membrane.

Membrane Damage & Hemolysis

Heinz bodies cause rigid "bite cells" as splenic macrophages remove inclusions. The damaged RBCs are destroyed intravascularly or extravascularly → acute hemolytic anemia.

07   Genetics & WHO Classification

X-Linked Inheritance & Variant Classification

The G6PD gene is located on the X chromosome (Xq28), encoding a 515-amino-acid protein. G6PD deficiency is X-linked recessive — males (hemizygous) are fully affected; females are carriers but may show intermediate phenotypes.

⚠️

X-Linked Pattern — Clinical Implication

Males (XY): One allele — fully expressed (affected or normal)  |  Females (XX): Two alleles — homozygous affected (rare, severe), heterozygous (carrier, usually asymptomatic but lyonization can cause variable expression)  |  Why G6PD deficiency persists: Heterozygous females may have relative malaria protection (positive selection in endemic regions).

08   Laboratory Diagnosis

Diagnosing G6PD Deficiency

Screening Tests

Fluorescent Spot Test (WHO-Recommended Screening)

G6PD-sufficient blood: G6P + NADP⁺ → NADPH, which fluoresces under UV light at 340 nm. G6PD-deficient: No fluorescence. Simple, cheap, suitable for field use.

Methemoglobin Reduction Test (Brewer Test)

G6PD-sufficient cells reduce methemoglobin to normal hemoglobin (blood turns red). G6PD-deficient cells fail to reduce it (blood remains brown). Qualitative screening.

Quantitative Spectrophotometric Assay (Gold Standard)

Measures G6PD activity in U/g Hb at 340 nm, monitoring NADPH formation rate. Results expressed as percentage of normal activity. Required for definitive diagnosis and Class classification.

Molecular / Genetic Testing

PCR-based mutation analysis identifies specific variants (e.g., G6PD A-: p.Asn126Asp; G6PD Mediterranean: p.Ser188Phe). Required for precise genotype-phenotype correlation and neonatal carrier detection.

⚠️

Critical Pre-Analytical Issue — False-Normal Results

Do NOT test G6PD levels during or immediately after an acute hemolytic episode. Older, G6PD-deficient RBCs are destroyed first; the remaining cells are younger reticulocytes with relatively higher G6PD activity, giving falsely near-normal results. Retest 6–8 weeks after the acute episode, once RBC population has normalized.

Key Takeaways

01
Identity
G6PD (EC 1.1.1.49) — oxidoreductase, rate-limiting enzyme of the PPP oxidative phase; NADP⁺-dependent; homodimer/homotetramer.
02
Reaction
G6P + NADP⁺ → 6-Phosphogluconolactone + NADPH + H⁺. Irreversible. Produces all NADPH in RBCs.
03
Function
NADPH regenerates GSH via glutathione reductase → protects RBCs from oxidative hemolysis. Also produces ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis.
04
Deficiency
Most common enzyme deficiency (~500M affected). X-linked recessive. Triggers: drugs (primaquine), foods (fava beans), infections. Heinz bodies + bite cells.
05
Classification
WHO Classes I–V by enzyme activity. Class II (G6PD Mediterranean) and Class III (G6PD A-) are most clinically relevant globally.
06
Diagnosis
Fluorescent spot test (screening) → Quantitative assay (gold standard, U/g Hb). Never test during acute hemolysis — false normal results. Retest after 6–8 weeks.
